On a glacial Tuesday in February, I went into the archive looking for old maps of Yale.

What I found instead were different imagined versions of Yale:

architectural plans, campus visions, proposed blueprints.

Some built,

some abandoned,

all revealing what kind of university Yale wanted to become.

Welcome to twentieth-century New Haven.

Yale's experiencing a lot of changes.

Along with these institutional changes, the university's campus was also a subject of change and redesign.

In 1919, John Russell Pope proposed a new master plan for Yale.

Look familiar?

New Haven... or Versailles?

Pope favored symmetry, interior gardens, and a formal center axis,

remniscent of European courts of the past.

These are not just maps of Yale.

They are allegories of Yale's intellectual and cultural aspirations.

Pope's plan suggests that a modern campus did not have to look "modern." For Yale, the future could be inherited from the prestige of European tradition.

Ultimately, Yale went with James Gamble Rogers' master plan, which still preserved many of Pope's Collegiate Gothic ideas.


Rogers went on to construct Sterling Memorial Library in 1927, which was met with backlash from the growing Modernist movement of the time. Many students complained the cathedral-like structure was overly historicist and lavish.

By the 1960s, Yale turned towards such Modernism with the construction of Morse and Stiles, departing from the Collegiate Gothic style.

Morse and Stiles were Brutalist in design, rejecting right angles in favor of irregular corners and raw concrete.

Their single-room layouts emphasized individualism.

Morse and Stiles imagined a Yale less tied to tradition and more willing to stand out.

By the early 1970s, Yale released plans for another pair of residential colleges.

These unnamed colleges were to be built at the Temple/Grove/Whitney triangular intersection.

Like Morse and Stiles, these colleges continued Yale's Brutalist turn.

These colleges were never built due to a confluence of financial, political, and preservationist interests.



When the new colleges finally came, they looked very different.

50 years pass.

In 2017, Yale opened Pauli Murray and Benjamin Franklin, its first new residential colleges in decades.

They were built at the northern edge of campus, near Prospect Street.

Construction timelapse.

The colleges possess slate roofs, limestone and brick facades, and stained-glass windows.

A gothic return.

"I think I'm leaving a legacy that I had good enough sense to climb on the shoulders of what went before, and to add to the legacy, not to go off in a new tangent."

— lead architect Robert A.M. Stern

This is the Yale we know today.

It is worth remembering the earlier visions of Yale, both realized and unrealized, that helped shape this campus.

Yale first looked to Europe for tradition and prestige.

Then it briefly tried to imagine itself differently.

Brutalism offered a more modern, defiant Yale.

But when Yale built again, it returned to traditional ideals.

The campus preserves these shifts in how the university saw itself.

By Elizabeth Schaefer.
